Reading
We're going to be giving you a fair bit of assigned reading.  This reading is gradually shaping itself into a single book for this class, but the shaping is still under way, so instead of giving you a single, slightly incoherent mass of reading, we're going to give you nice, logical chunks of reading.  Aren't we nice? 

But wait, there's more!  We'll be providing you with these readings on NB, a system that allows you to ask questions, make comments, and answer each other's questions about the reading.  Pretty amazing, no?!?  
And if that wasn't nice enough, we're going to give you these readings for the low, low price of NOTHING.  Nada.  Free of charge.

There's got to be a catch, right?

Well, yes, there is.  Two catches, actually.  First, when we assign you reading, we expect you to make comments, ask questions, and/or answer questions on the reading.  In other words, part of the reading assignment is to comment on the text.  This has a few purposes:  First, it helps your learning, because it requires you to engage actively with the reading, and it allows you to ask questions.  Second, it helps your classmates' learning, since often students' answers are as good or better than instructors'.  Finally, it allows us to identify which parts of this book need work -- we need feedback to make it better, and there is no better feedback than the questions and observations of students who are actually using the thing.

When we say that we expect you to make comments, ask questions, and answer questions, this does not mean we want you to make silly comments or ask obvious questions just for the sake of doing so.  We're not going to be grading you on how many comments you make.  We just expect you to really engage with the reading.

One other note about reading:  Many of the readings deal with learning to do things (for example, work with MATLAB).  By itself, reading does not make you learn to do things -- rather, you need actually to DO the thing.   Therefore, the way you read this material should be different from the way you read a novel.  Rather than relaxing on a couch, sipping tea, and casually contemplating the beauty of modeling as you read, you should be trying stuff out on your computer or on paper as you read.  Much of the reading is written in a way that encourages you to do exactly this; you'll find you learn a lot more from the reading if you try the examples, etc. as you read.  Yes, this will take longer.  But it will be oh-so-satisfying.  And if you don't, and you ask the ninjas questions that are clearly addressed in the reading, expect a somewhat skeptical "Did you do the reading?" in response to your question.

Class Meetings
We will generally have two kinds of class meetings:  "tutorials", which are held in the auditorium, and which introduce new concepts through active lectures, short small group activities, etc; and "studios", which are held in the design studios, and which provide the opportunity for you to work in more depth on either assignments or projects for the course.  We wish that we could tell you now exactly which days we'll be in the auditorium, but typically we adjust as we go along.

Deliverables
You will turn in a variety of stuff for this class.  We call these things "deliverables".

1. In-Class Diagnostics
We will, on a fairly regular basis, give you short in-class diagnostics.  Think of these as quick, check-in quizzes -- we expect these to typically take about 10 minutes; they are meant to help you make sure you have stuff under control.

2. Out-of-Class Problem Sets
We will also often give you somewhat meatier out-of-class assignments that require more in-depth work and understanding the material.  These problem sets will generally be done outside of class time, although we will sometimes devote some studio time to them.  Problem sets will graded in a binary fashion -- they are either checked off as correct, or they are not.  If you do not complete the problem set correctly at first, you are free to work on it some more until you get it right, and then get it checked off only once you have completed it correctly.

While we encourage you to discuss problem sets with your colleagues, they are intended to provide a way to check that you individually understand the material.  So, while it is fine to talk about how to approach a problem set, to look at someone's code, and to collaboratively work through a problem at the whiteboard, in the end you should only turn in work that you can reproduce and explain on your own.
3. Projects
Over the course of the semester you will do three projects.  The first project is pretty heavily structured, and will be done individually (see comment above about individual work).  The second and third project will be done in pairs, or occasionally by individuals or threes.  

These projects are intended to be explorations of topics connected to the major theme for a given portion of the semester. Each project is intended to provide an opportunity for students to pose and solve an authentic problem of their own choosing. In addition, each project provides an opportunity for students to apply basic disciplinary knowledge and strengthen their skills in modeling and simulation.  Each project ends with the creation of a material that graphically communicates your work (either poster elements or a poster), as well as (for projects 2 and 3) a formal presentation, in the style of a poster session at a scientific conference.  Both the technical quality of your work and the professionalism of your presentation will be assessed.
 

Turning Things In

With the exception of in-class diagnostics (which you just need to get checked off for), you should turn in all problem sets and project deliverables in as a PDF or image file, following the instructions in "Turning in work."

In-Class Diagnostics: In-class diagnostics must be checked off in class by showing your results to the instructor or ninja.  If your results are correct, you’ll be told, “congrats, you’re done!”  If there’s some problem with your answers, we’ll give you some suggestions, and tell you to work on it some more.

Out-of-Class Problem Sets: Problem sets must be completed and uploaded to the appropriate folder by the assigned due date and time (usually 10:00 pm). You must get your problem set "checked off" by a ninja before you upload it. Ninjas will schedule office hours on the nights preceding the due dates, and you are encouraged to work with ninjas both to get help and to get your problem sets checked off. You are STRONGLY encouraged to get problem sets checked off early to avoid the rush. By uploading your completed problem set to the directory, you are confirming that the work is yours, and that it has been checked off by a ninja.

Please upload each problem set as a SINGLE .pdf or image file. That might mean copying your code and final graphs into a word doc and then turning that into a pdf, or scanning some handwritten work, or something more creative than that.   Note that this means that if you’re turning in code, the code has to be legible by a human being.

Projects: You are working in a studio space so that you can take advantage of the ability to post your work – making physical representations of your ideas, and using those to inform your subsequent work, is a really handy approach.  Interim and final deliverables for projects should be posted in your studio space (you may cover things over as they get old, or move things up high on the wall, etc.).  In addition, you should upload any assigned project deliverables to the appropriate folder in the directory structure.


Grading
This course is pass-no record; we will give you feedback about things, and give you a sense of how well you are doing at things, but we will not give you a grade. 

We will determine your performance in the course based on Problem Sets (35%), Projects (45%) and Professionalism (20%). "Professionalism" includes, but is not limited to, completion of interim deliverables, contribution to class discussions, completion of assigned reading, playing well with others, and good personal hygiene.  You can check in with a ninja at any time to see what your current status is; we'll also harass you if we have any concerns about how things are going.

Competencies
This course will provide development and assessment of the following competencies: 
  • Oral and Visual Communication
  • Qualitative Analysis
  • Quantitative Analysis
  • Posing and Testing Hypotheses
Honor Code
Your work in this course is subject to the Olin College Honor Code.  In particular, you are expected only to hand in work that you contributed to substantively, can explain, can reproduce (see comments above about collaboration), and (in the case of diagnostics) have had checked off by a ninja or an instructor.  It is not acceptable to hand in work that you cannot explain and reproduce (for example, it is not OK to give a presentation about a simulation that you don’t understand!).
